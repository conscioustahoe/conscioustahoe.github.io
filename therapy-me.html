Introducing therapy.me

therapy.me lets people do personalized cognitive behavioural therapy without a therapist. CBT is a science-backed, goal-oriented, structured and highly effective treatment for anxiety and depression, but most people can't afford a therapist to administer it.

therapy.me will assess a users needs, teach them relevant CBT concepts, walk them through interactive exercises, keep them motivated, and measure their progress.

Anxiety and depression are pervasive in the tech culture. A culture defined by unrelenting pressure, high stakes and lack of humility. To survive in this ecosystem, you have to show that you're calm, composed and in control; that you're smoothly gliding through the water; you don’t show how furiously your legs are kicking and struggling underneath.

That's the Stanford Duck Syndrome. Ducks in a pond appear to glide easily through the surface of the water and look elegant while doing so; beneath the surface they are paddling fiercely. Perhaps it’s because we shy away from talking about our problems and show any humility because it might be percieved as a sign weakness and vulnerability by our peers.

Cognitive behavioural therapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a type of psychotherapy that has been shown to be highly effective in treating anxiety and depression. CBT is based on the idea that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are all interconnected. When we have negative thoughts, it can lead to negative feelings and behaviors. CBT helps us to identify and challenge our negative thoughts, and to develop more helpful and realistic ways of thinking. For this reason CBT is incredibly well suited for engineers as it mirrors our world view and how we approach problems.

Thought log

Let's look at a method of treatment known as the thought log to understand the striking similarities between engineers and CBT:

  • Identify a recent situation that caused anxiety
  • Log the thought(s) you had (e.g., "I'm a complete imposter in this world of genius and talent")
  • Record the emotions you felt and rate their intensity (e.g., shame 90%, anxious 70%, sadness 50%)
  • Find the "bugs" in your thinking (e.g., all-or-nothing thinking, or seeing things as completely good or bad; labeling, e.g., because I made a mistake, I must be a loser; not giving oneself credit, or thinking positive things in your life are just luck or someone else's doing)
  • Generate a more accurate thought (e.g., "Everyone fucks up every now and then. I've seen the smartest people on my team make worse mistakes.")
  • Re-record and re-rate your emotions
  • Re-assess changes in your emotions (e.g., shame 40%, anxiety 30%, sadness 50%)
